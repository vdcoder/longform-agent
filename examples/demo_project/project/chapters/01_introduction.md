# Introduction

Software teams fail in predictable ways. Deadlines slip, quality erodes, and
talented engineers leave for jobs that feel less chaotic. Managers respond with
more process, more meetings, and stricter deadlines — which usually makes
things worse.

This book argues that reliability is not a property of processes or tools. It is
a property of teams. A reliable team ships predictably, recovers from incidents
without blame, and maintains the codebase well enough to keep delivering value
years into the future. Everything else follows from that.

## What We Mean by Reliability

A reliable software team has three properties:

1. **Predictability.** Estimates are roughly accurate. Commitments are met or
   renegotiated early. Stakeholders trust the team's word.
2. **Resilience.** When things go wrong — and they will — the team responds
   calmly, learns from failures, and avoids repeat incidents.
3. **Sustainability.** The team can maintain its output over months and years
   without burning people out or letting the codebase decay.

These properties reinforce each other. Predictability reduces the pressure that
causes corners to be cut. Resilience reduces the fear that makes teams hide
problems. Sustainability gives engineers the time to do work they are proud of.

## Why Teams Fail

Most software team failures have a common structure: short-term pressure crowds
out long-term investment. A deadline causes a shortcut. The shortcut slows down
the next feature. The next feature's deadline causes two more shortcuts. Over
time, the team spends most of its energy managing the consequences of past
decisions rather than building new things.

This is not a failure of individual engineers. It is a failure of system design.
Your job as a manager or lead is to design a system — a team, a process, a
culture — that resists this pattern.

## How This Book Is Organised

The chapters that follow address three domains where reliability is built or lost:

- **Technical debt** (Chapter 2): how to name it, measure it, and reduce it
  without stopping product work.
- **On-call culture** (Chapter 3): how to design a rotation that is sustainable
  and a review process that produces learning rather than blame.

Each chapter ends with a Key Takeaways section and a short set of questions to
help you apply the ideas to your own team.

## Key Takeaways

- Reliability is a team property, not a process property.
- The three components of reliability are predictability, resilience, and
  sustainability.
- Most team failures follow the same pattern: short-term pressure crowds out
  long-term investment.
- Your job is to design a system that resists that pattern.
